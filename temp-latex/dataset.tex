\section{Introduction to the CIC-IDS2018 Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset-intro}
% ============================================================

The dataset used in the experimental phase of this work is the
\textbf{CIC-IDS2018} (Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity — Intrusion
Detection System 2018), a publicly available benchmark released by the
University of New Brunswick~\cite{cicids2018}.  It was produced as part of a
systematic effort to build realistic, labelled network-traffic traces that can
drive the development and evaluation of next-generation intrusion-detection
systems (IDS).

The data were collected over a five-day period in February–March 2018 inside a
controlled cyber-range infrastructure.  A set of attack machines (an external
network of 50 machines) launched a curated battery of attacks against a victim
network of 420 machines and 30 servers, while the defender-side profile
generator replayed realistic benign traffic drawn from a profiled usage model
that incorporates HTTP, HTTPS, FTP, SSH, and email activity.  All bi-directional
network flows were captured with the \textit{CIC\-Flow\-Meter} tool, which
extracts statistical features — primarily packet-level and flow-level statistics
— from raw \texttt{.pcap} dumps.  Each flow is labelled with either
\texttt{Benign} or one of fourteen mutually exclusive attack categories.

The attack repertoire covers a broad spectrum of modern threat types:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Brute-Force} attacks (FTP-BruteForce, SSH-BruteForce);
    \item \textbf{Denial-of-Service (DoS)} attacks (DoS-GoldenEye,
          DoS-Hulk, DoS-SlowHTTPTest, DoS-Slowloris);
    \item \textbf{Web attacks} (SQL Injection, XSS, Brute-Force on web
          login);
    \item \textbf{Botnet} traffic (ARES botnet);
    \item \textbf{Infiltration} scenarios;
    \item \textbf{Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS)} attacks with LOIC
          UDP and HOIC.
\end{itemize}

The dataset was originally distributed as a collection of day-labelled CSV
files, one per day of capture.  A preprocessing step prior to the analysis
described in this chapter merged these individual files into a unified dataset
and resolved a minor schema inconsistency: four features present only in one
daily capture were not shared across all files; rather than discarding those
flows, the missing columns were filled with \texttt{NaN} and the anomalous
features were saved separately so that no information was irrecoverably lost.
The unified dataset was subsequently persisted in the Apache Parquet columnar
format to enable efficient distributed reads via Apache Spark in the
experimental pipeline.

The dataset is available for download from the Kaggle platform at
\url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/solarmainframe/ids-intrusion-csv}, while
additional documentation and the original raw captures are hosted at
\url{https://www.unb.ca/cic/datasets/ids-2018.html}.

\section{Dimensionality and Schema}
\label{sec:dataset-dimensionality}
% ============================================================

After unification, the dataset contains \textbf{16\,173\,208 network-flow
instances} described by \textbf{79 features} plus two label columns
(\texttt{Label} and \texttt{Label\_generic}).
The 79 features are purely statistical descriptors extracted by CICFlowMeter;
they include, among others:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Flow duration and inter-arrival times} (\texttt{Flow Duration},
        \texttt{Flow IAT Mean}, \texttt{Flow IAT Std}, etc.);
  \item \emph{Packet-length statistics} (\texttt{Fwd Pkt Len Mean},
        \texttt{Bwd Pkt Len Max}, \texttt{Pkt Len Var}, etc.);
  \item \emph{Byte and packet counts}, split by direction (\texttt{Tot Fwd
        Pkts}, \texttt{Tot Bwd Pkts}, \texttt{TotLen Fwd Pkts}, etc.);
  \item \emph{TCP flag counts} (\texttt{SYN Flag Cnt}, \texttt{FIN Flag
        Cnt}, \texttt{PSH Flag Cnt}, etc.);
  \item \emph{Rate-based features} (\texttt{Flow Byts/s}, \texttt{Flow
        Pkts/s}, \texttt{Bwd Pkts/s}, etc.);
  \item \emph{Sub-flow and bulk statistics} (\texttt{Subflow Fwd Pkts},
        \texttt{Fwd Blk Rate Avg}, etc.);
  \item \emph{Window size and header-length fields}
        (\texttt{Init\_Win\_bytes\_forward}, \texttt{Fwd Header Len}, etc.).
\end{itemize}

Crucially, the dataset contains \emph{no application-layer payload
information}: all features are derived solely from transport- and
network-layer flow metadata.  This design choice, motivated by privacy
regulations that preclude deep-packet inspection on real traffic, constitutes an
inherent upper bound on the discriminative power available to any classifier
trained on these data, a limitation that will be revisited when analysing
feature–target correlations (Section~\ref{sec:feat-target-corr}).

\section{Label Distribution and Class Imbalance}
\label{sec:label-distribution}
% ============================================================

A preliminary examination of the label columns reveals a severe class
imbalance, a characteristic endemic to real-world network-traffic datasets.
The label schema is twofold:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{Label} — the original fine-grained label carrying the specific
        attack category or \texttt{Benign}.
  \item \texttt{Label\_generic} — a binary encoding derived from
        \texttt{Label}: $0$ for benign flows and $1$ for any attack flow,
        regardless of type.  This binarised label was introduced to simplify
        the experimental evaluation of deep learning models in scenarios where
        the primary objective is anomaly detection rather than attack
        classification.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:label-distribution} shows the joint distribution of both label
hierarchies.  The left pie chart illustrates the overall fraction of benign
versus malign traffic, while the right chart details the relative proportions
of the individual attack categories within the malign subset.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/label_distribution_piecharts.png}
  \caption{Class distribution of the CIC-IDS2018 dataset.  \emph{Left:}
    proportion of benign versus malign flows (binary label).  \emph{Right:}
    breakdown of specific attack categories within the malign portion.}
  \label{fig:label-distribution}
\end{figure}

The imbalance is substantial: benign traffic constitutes the overwhelming
majority of the dataset, while the aggregate of all attack types represents a
considerably smaller fraction.  Within the attack classes, further heterogeneity
is observed: volumetric attacks such as DoS/DDoS generate large numbers of
flows and are therefore over-represented relative to low-rate, stealthy attacks
such as infiltration or web-based brute force.

Class imbalance has well-documented implications for machine learning model
training~\cite{lemaitre2017imbalanced}: standard cross-entropy minimisation
tends to bias models towards the majority class, artificially inflating accuracy
while degrading recall on minority attack categories.  Awareness of this
imbalance was therefore a prerequisite for designing the experimental
protocol, and it also motivates the particular interest in \emph{noise
injection} experiments: the introduction of synthetic corruptions by PuckTrick
may interact non-uniformly with class-stratified data, potentially exacerbating
or alleviating the imbalance in ways that influence classifier behaviour in
non-trivial ways.

\section{Temporal Distribution of Traffic}
\label{sec:temporal-distribution}
% ============================================================

Each flow record includes a \texttt{Timestamp} column that encodes the
start time of the flow as a character string.  A thorough inspection of this
column revealed that multiple date-format conventions coexist across daily
files (\texttt{yyyy-MM-dd HH:mm:ss}, \texttt{dd/MM/yyyy HH:mm:ss}, and
variants thereof), a direct consequence of data originating from independently
configured capture tools.  Additionally, a non-negligible fraction of rows
contained epoch-origin timestamps (date 1 January 1970), a classical artefact
of integer overflow or uninitialised timestamp fields.  These corrupted entries
were filtered out before temporal analysis was performed.

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-distribution} presents the temporal structure of the
dataset along two complementary dimensions.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/temporal_distribution_label_generic.png}
  \caption{Temporal distribution of network flows.  \emph{Top:}
    stacked area chart showing the hourly volume of benign (steelblue) and
    malign (tomato) flows over the entire five-day collection window.
    \emph{Bottom:} aggregated distribution of flow counts by hour of the day
    (0--23), with separate bars for benign and malign traffic.}
  \label{fig:temporal-distribution}
\end{figure}

The stacked area chart (top panel) reveals that attacks are not uniformly
spread across the collection period but are concentrated in specific temporal
windows.  This clustering reflects the scripted nature of the attack sessions:
attack tools were activated according to a defined schedule, generating bursts
of malign traffic interspersed with periods of exclusively benign activity.

The hourly aggregation chart (bottom panel) provides a time-of-day analysis.
Benign traffic follows a circadian pattern consistent with daytime office
usage, peaking during working hours, while malign traffic exhibits a more
uniform or counter-cyclical distribution depending on the attack type.  These
temporal patterns imply that the \texttt{Timestamp} feature carries genuine
discriminative information: a classifier with access to flow timing can
leverage temporal context to improve attack detection.

From the standpoint of PuckTrick's noise injection experiments, this
observation is significant: the \texttt{Timestamp} column is a textual
categorical feature whose corruption — via label inconsistency or noise
injection — can degrade the temporal signal exploitable by downstream models,
making it an especially sensitive target for controlled perturbation experiments.

\section{Missing Data Analysis}
\label{sec:missing-data}
% ============================================================

Prior to any modelling activity, the completeness of the feature matrix was
assessed by counting null and \texttt{NaN} values across all 79 columns.
The initial scan indicated no missing values in the unified dataset — a result
that might suggest the data are entirely complete.  However, a more granular
investigation revealed that one feature contained a small proportion of missing
values (roughly 0.37\% of all rows).

Given that 0.37\% represents a negligible fraction of a dataset containing
over 16 million instances, the standard remediation strategy of \emph{listwise
deletion} — i.e., discarding all rows that contain at least one null value — was
adopted.  This approach is justified because:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The lost rows amount to approximately 60\,000 instances, less than
        0.4\% of the total, which is statistically negligible.
  \item The missingness pattern showed no systematic relationship with the
        target variable; the null-bearing flows were distributed across both
        benign and attack classes, indicating a process closer to
        \emph{Missing Completely At Random} (MCAR) rather than a
        class-specific artefact.
  \item Imputation strategies (mean, median, or model-based) would introduce
        artificial values into flow-level measurement features, potentially
        contaminating the baseline dataset against which noise-injection
        experiments are benchmarked.
\end{enumerate}

After listwise deletion the dataset retained all 16\,173\,208 instances that
carried complete feature vectors, and no further missing-data handling was
necessary.

\section{Feature Relevance Analysis}
\label{sec:feature-relevance}
% ============================================================

A fundamental step before introducing controlled noise is to understand the
informative content of each feature and the relationships among features.
This section describes the sequence of analyses performed to characterise the
feature space, identify redundancies, understand distributional properties, and
quantify each feature's relationship with the target variable.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Bivariate Correlation Analysis and Redundant Features}
\label{sec:corr-analysis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

High pairwise correlations among features indicate redundancy: if two features
carry essentially the same information, retaining both inflates computational
cost without increasing the model's discriminative capacity.  Moreover,
redundant features can interact adversely with regularisation mechanisms and
complicate the interpretation of noise-injection effects (corrupting one feature
in a highly correlated pair may partially mask the corruption through the
surviving redundant feature).

To systematically identify redundant features, a Pearson correlation matrix was
computed over all 79 numeric features using Apache Spark's distributed
\texttt{Correlation.corr()} function.  The result is a $79 \times 79$ symmetric
matrix whose entry $(i,j)$ is the sample Pearson correlation coefficient between
features $i$ and $j$.  Figure~\ref{fig:correlation-heatmap} renders this matrix
as a heatmap using a diverging colour scale centred at zero.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/feature-correlation-heatmap.png}
  \caption{Pearson correlation heatmap for all 79 numeric features of the
    CIC-IDS2018 dataset.  Warm colours indicate strong positive correlation,
    cool colours indicate strong negative correlation, and white indicates
    near-zero correlation.  Visible block structures correspond to groups of
    semantically related flow statistics.}
  \label{fig:correlation-heatmap}
\end{figure}

The heatmap reveals pronounced block-diagonal structures, indicative of groups
of semantically related features (e.g., forward-direction statistics, backward-
direction statistics, sub-flow aggregations) that are strongly inter-correlated
within their group.

A threshold of $|r| \geq 0.95$ was adopted as the criterion for declaring a
feature pair redundant, following the practice common in feature selection for
IDS datasets~\cite{gharib2016evaluation}.  Under this criterion,
\textbf{46 feature pairs} were identified as highly correlated.  Table~\ref{tab:high-corr-pairs-examples} lists a representative sample.

\begin{table}[htbp]

  \label{tab:high-corr-pairs-examples}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{llr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Feature A} & \textbf{Feature B} & \textbf{$r$} \\
    \midrule
    Flow Duration      & Fwd IAT Tot        & 1.000 \\
    TotLen Bwd Pkts    & Subflow Bwd Byts   & 1.000 \\
    Fwd Header Len     & Bwd Header Len     & 0.999 \\
    Pkt Len Mean       & Fwd Pkt Len Mean   & 0.999 \\
    Tot Fwd Pkts       & Subflow Fwd Pkts   & 1.000 \\
    TotLen Fwd Pkts    & Subflow Fwd Byts   & 1.000 \\
    Flow IAT Std       & Fwd IAT Std        & 0.991 \\

    Fwd Act Data Pkts  & Tot Fwd Pkts       & 0.967 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

From each correlated pair, the feature deemed to carry less distinctive semantic
meaning (e.g.\ sub-flow aggregates that duplicate full-flow counts, or
header-length terms that duplicate packet-length terms) was designated as a
removal candidate.  This process yielded \textbf{27 candidate features for
removal} due to high inter-feature correlation.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Zero-Variance Features}
\label{sec:zero-variance}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

A separate screening pass computed the standard deviation of every feature over
the entire dataset.  Features with a standard deviation of exactly zero are
\emph{constant} — they take the same value for every flow — and therefore
provide no discriminative information whatsoever.  Including them in a model
wastes memory and computational resources and may cause numerical difficulties
in distance-based or gradient-based algorithms.

The screening identified \textbf{8 zero-variance features}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{Bwd Blk Rate Avg}
  \item \texttt{Bwd Byts/b Avg}
  \item \texttt{Bwd PSH Flags}
  \item \texttt{Bwd Pkts/b Avg}
  \item \texttt{Bwd URG Flags}
  \item \texttt{Fwd Blk Rate Avg}
  \item \texttt{Fwd Byts/b Avg}
  \item \texttt{Fwd Pkts/b Avg}
\end{itemize}

All eight features are CICFlowMeter bulk-transfer statistics that are uniformly
zero in this capture environment, presumably because the network configuration
or the flow-generator version used did not populate these fields.  Their removal
is unconditional.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Reduced Feature Set}
\label{sec:reduced-feature-set}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

After removing the 8 zero-variance features and the 27 features flagged as
redundant by the high-correlation criterion, the dataset retains
\textbf{43 numeric features} plus the two label columns.  This reduced feature
set is the basis for all subsequent analyses in this chapter and for the
noise-injection experiments described in Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Feature Type Classification}
\label{sec:feature-types}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Not all of the 43 remaining features have the same measurement scale, and this
distinction is directly relevant to PuckTrick's noise-injection modules.
Gaussian noise — appropriate for continuous-valued features — is semantically
inappropriate when applied to a binary or low-cardinality categorical feature,
just as label-shuffling corruption is ill-suited to a continuous measurement.
To ensure that noise strategies are applied correctly, each feature was
classified by its \emph{effective cardinality} (the number of distinct observed
values):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Binary} (cardinality $= 2$): flag counters that are either on
        or off for a given flow, e.g.\  \texttt{SYN Flag Cnt},
        \texttt{FIN Flag Cnt}.
  \item \textbf{Categorical} (cardinality between 3 and 200): low-cardinality
        features such as \texttt{Protocol} (three distinct values: TCP, UDP,
        ICMP encoded as integers) and window-size step features.
  \item \textbf{Continuous} (cardinality $> 200$): all high-resolution
        measurement features (durations, byte counts, inter-arrival times,
        rates, etc.).
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:categorical-features} presents the cardinality distribution
across features.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/categorical_binary_features_analysis.png}
  \caption{Feature type classification for the 43-feature reduced set.  The
    bar chart (left) shows the number of distinct values per feature for
    low-cardinality features; the pie chart (right) summarises the proportion
    of binary, categorical, and continuous features.}
  \label{fig:categorical-features}
\end{figure}

The final breakdown of the 43 features is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{4 binary} features;
  \item \textbf{2 categorical} (low-cardinality) features;
  \item \textbf{30 continuous} features (the remaining 7 features include
        additional binary or categorical items depending on the specific
        cardinality threshold applied).
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Feature Distribution Analysis: Skewness and Kurtosis}
\label{sec:skew-kurtosis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Understanding the distributional shape of each continuous feature is essential
for predicting how noise injection will affect data quality.  Two distributional
moments are particularly informative in this context:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Skewness} measures the asymmetry of the distribution.  A
        skewness close to zero indicates an approximately symmetric
        distribution; large positive values indicate a long right tail
        (most values are small but a few are extremely large); large negative
        values indicate the converse.  For noise injection, high positive
        skewness is especially problematic: adding zero-mean Gaussian noise
        to a right-skewed feature can displace values below zero — a physically
        meaningless outcome for quantities such as packet counts or byte totals
        that are strictly non-negative.
  \item \textbf{Kurtosis} (excess kurtosis, relative to the Gaussian baseline
        of 0) measures the heaviness of the distribution's tails.  High
        positive kurtosis implies that extreme values (outliers) are far more
        frequent than in a normal distribution.  Injecting Gaussian noise into
        a heavy-tailed feature risks corrupting exactly those extreme values
        that are most discriminative for detecting volumetric attacks (which
        manifest precisely as statistical outliers in byte-count and rate
        features).
\end{itemize}

Descriptive statistics — mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, skewness,
and kurtosis — were computed for all 42 numeric features (one feature,
\texttt{Protocol}, is categorical and was excluded from this step) using
Spark's distributed aggregation functions.  Figure~\ref{fig:skew-kurtosis}
displays skewness and kurtosis as side-by-side horizontal bar charts, with bars
exceeding the threshold of $|{}\text{skewness}{}| > 2$ and $\text{kurtosis} >
7$ highlighted in red to indicate features of concern.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/feature-skew-kurtosis.png}
  \caption{Skewness (left) and excess kurtosis (right) for the 42 numeric
    features.  Red bars indicate values exceeding the fragility threshold
    ($|\text{skewness}| > 2$ and kurtosis $> 7$, respectively), signalling
    features likely to be significantly affected by additive Gaussian noise.}
  \label{fig:skew-kurtosis}
\end{figure}

The analysis reveals an extreme distributional asymmetry prevalent across the
feature set.  Notable examples include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{Pkt Len Var}: skewness $\approx 1863.6$, kurtosis
        $\approx 3\,600\,000$ — the most extreme case;
  \item \texttt{TotLen Fwd Pkts}: skewness $\approx 836.7$, indicating a
        distribution dominated by very small values (short flows) with an
        extended right tail of large data transfers;
  \item \texttt{Tot Bwd Pkts}: skewness $\approx 170.8$;
  \item \texttt{Flow Byts/s} and \texttt{Flow Pkts/s}: these rate features
        exhibit numerically infinite values due to zero-duration flows (a
        division-by-zero artefact of the flow meter), which must be handled
        carefully prior to any noise injection.
\end{itemize}

In total, \textbf{34 out of 42 features} exhibit $|\text{skewness}| > 2$,
classifying the overwhelming majority of the CIC-IDS2018 feature set as
highly non-Gaussian.

% ............................................................
\subsubsection{Feature Fragility Classification}
\label{sec:fragility}
% ............................................................

Based on the joint evaluation of skewness and kurtosis, each continuous feature
was assigned a \emph{fragility level} indicating its susceptibility to
statistical distortion by PuckTrick's noise-injection procedures:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{High fragility} (high skewness \emph{and} high kurtosis):
        features whose distributions are both asymmetric and heavy-tailed.
        Gaussian noise will simultaneously generate invalid negative values and
        disrupt the discriminative outlier structure.
  \item \textbf{Medium fragility} (high skewness \emph{or} high kurtosis but
        not both): features with one adverse distributional property but not
        the other.
  \item \textbf{Low fragility} (robust): features whose distributions are
        approximately symmetric and have near-Gaussian tails; Gaussian noise
        injection is both statistically appropriate and unlikely to produce
        artefactual values.
\end{itemize}

The classification yields:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{30 high-fragility} features;
  \item \textbf{5 medium-fragility} features;
  \item \textbf{7 low-fragility (robust)} features.
\end{itemize}

Representative examples from each fragility class are shown in

\subsection{Feature–Target Correlation Analysis}
\label{sec:feat-target-corr}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Having characterised the intra-feature structure and distributional properties,
the final step of the dataset analysis quantifies the relationship between each
feature and the target variable.  Two complementary statistical measures were
employed, one for each level of the label hierarchy:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Point-biserial correlation with \texttt{Label\_generic}
        (binary target):} the Pearson correlation coefficient between a
        continuous feature and the binary $\{0, 1\}$ encoded target is
        mathematically equivalent to the point-biserial correlation
        coefficient.  It measures how much the feature's mean differs between
        benign and malign flows, normalised by the overall variability.
  \item \textbf{ANOVA F-statistic with \texttt{Label} (multi-class target):}
        the one-way analysis of variance F-statistic measures the ratio of
        between-class variance to within-class variance.  A high F-statistic
        indicates that a feature takes substantially different mean values
        across the individual attack categories, making it useful for
        fine-grained attack classification.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:target-correlation} presents the two rankings side by side,
colour-coded by the fragility class defined in
Section~\ref{sec:fragility}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/fragile-features-target-correlation.png}
  \caption{Feature relevance with respect to the target variable.
    \emph{Left:} features ranked by $|\rho|$ (absolute Pearson correlation)
    with the binary \texttt{Label\_generic}.
    \emph{Right:} features ranked by the ANOVA F-statistic with the
    multi-class \texttt{Label} (log scale).
    Colour coding: red = high fragility, orange = medium fragility,
    green = low fragility.}
  \label{fig:target-correlation}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Correlation with the binary label.}
The feature exhibiting the highest absolute correlation with
\texttt{Label\_generic} is \texttt{Fwd Seg Size Min} ($|\rho| \approx 0.43$),
followed by \texttt{Bwd Pkts/s} ($|\rho| \approx 0.26$), \texttt{Protocol}
($|\rho| \approx 0.23$), and \texttt{Fwd Pkt Len Mean} ($|\rho| \approx 0.23$).
Crucially, the \emph{maximum} observed correlation is approximately 0.43 — a
modest value that places an inherent limit on how well any linear or
correlation-based model can separate the two classes using a single feature.
This is a direct consequence of the absence of payload-level information: since
the dataset contains only transport-layer statistics measured on encrypted or
high-volume traffic, no individual feature provides a sharp decision boundary.

\paragraph{Discrimination across attack types (ANOVA).}
The ANOVA analysis paints a complementary picture.  Features such as
\texttt{Tot Fwd Pkts} (F $\approx 2.0 \times 10^6$), \texttt{Bwd Pkts/s} (F
$\approx 1.8 \times 10^6$), and \texttt{TotLen Fwd Pkts} (F $\approx 7.4
\times 10^5$) exhibit extremely high between-class variance relative to
within-class variance, indicating that they are excellent discriminators
\emph{across attack categories}, even though their point-biserial correlations
with the binary label are moderate.  This asymmetry arises because different
attack types produce qualitatively different flow profiles (e.g., DoS floods
generate enormous packet counts while brute-force attacks generate numerous
short flows), yet all attack flows collectively differ from benign flows in a
more diffuse, multi-dimensional fashion.

\paragraph{Fragility vs.\ relevance tension.}
A particularly significant observation emerging from
Figure~\ref{fig:target-correlation} is that the most discriminative features
tend to fall in the \emph{high-fragility} category (red bars).  This creates a
direct tension with noise injection: the features that contribute most to
classifier performance are precisely the features whose distributions are most
vulnerable to distortion by Gaussian noise.  This tension is one of the central
experimental questions addressed in Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}: can deep
learning models maintain their discriminative capability when their most
informative-yet-fragile features are corrupted by PuckTrick?

The analysis presented in this chapter establishes the informational context
necessary to interpret the noise-injection experiments.  The following points
are especially relevant for subsequent chapters:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \textbf{severe class imbalance} requires that model performance be
        evaluated with metrics beyond accuracy (e.g., F1-score,
        precision-recall curves) and that the impact of noise be assessed
        class-conditionally.
  \item The \textbf{extreme distributional skewness} of most features implies
        that PuckTrick's Gaussian noise module will, in many cases, violate
        the non-negativity constraints of the measurement domain.  This makes
        the observed performance degradation attributable not only to the
        statistical displacement of feature values but also to the creation of
        physically invalid instances.
  \item The \textbf{fragility–relevance tension} — the fact that the most
        predictive features are also the most fragile — means that even
        moderate noise magnitudes can have disproportionate effects on model
        performance, a hypothesis central to the experimental design.
  \item The \textbf{temporal signal} embedded in the \texttt{Timestamp}
        column provides contextual information that complements the
        flow-statistics features.  Its corruption under PuckTrick's label
        inconsistency module constitutes a distinct and interesting experimental
        scenario.
\end{enumerate}

Taken together, these properties render the CIC-IDS2018 dataset a demanding and
realistic testbed for evaluating the effects of controlled data corruption on
deep learning-based intrusion detection systems.
